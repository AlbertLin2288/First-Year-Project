\section{Invariant subspaces}
\lettrine{W}{e}
usually consider a linear map $T:V\rightarrow V$, where $V$ is finite dimensional. For a random subspace $S\subset V$, its image under $T$ is usually also random.
However we consider those $S$ which do not change under $T$, called invariant subspaces:$$v\in S\implies T(v)\in S$$
For example, the well-known subspace $$E_\lambda = \{v: T(v)=\lambda v\}$$ is invariant - each element is scaled under $T$, which is still in $E_\lambda$.
$\\$$\\$ The image $$\im T=\{T(v): v\in V\}$$is invariant almost by definition.$\\$$\\$ 
Another example, important for later work, is the generalized kernel $$K=\{v: \exists k\in \mathbb{N}^* \text{ s.t } T^k(v)=0\}$$
This is indeed a subspace, and the invariance here follows from picking $v\in K$ nonzero and it's corresponding $k$ such that we have 
$$T^k(v)=0\implies T^{k-1}(T(v))=0\implies T(v)\in K$$.
\hfill $(\heartsuit)$ \\ \\ 
\subsection{}
Invariant subspaces (except $\{\mathbf{0}\}$) $S$ under $T$ have an important corollary - they all contain an eigenvector of $T$. This is for the simple reason that the linear map

 $$T^*:S\rightarrow S\ \text{,} \ T^*(v)=T(v)$$   
is well defined due to the invariance. Then we just pick the eigenvector of $T^*$ (always exists).

\section{Eigenspaces and null-chains}
In search of a canonical (unique $+$ nice) matrix representation of any $T$. 
Lets pick our beautiful eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2,...,\lambda_k$ for an linear operator $T$ over $V$.
We have the eigenspaces $$E_{\lambda_{i}}=\{v: T(v)=\lambda_i v\}$$
It is important to know that $$E_{\lambda_i}\cap E_{\lambda_j}=\{0\}  \ \text {, for all }i<j\leq k,$$and that for an arbitrary basis $\beta_i$ for $E_{\lambda_i}$, the set $$\bigcup_{i=1}^{k} \beta_i$$is linearly independent.$\newline$$\newline$
One may wonder: how nice would it be if $\bigoplus_{i=1}^{k} E_{\lambda_i}$ spans the whole space - then $\bigcup \beta_i$ may be our basis. 
Sadly, this is often not the case. An example is the matrix \[
A = \begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 1 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\]
with its corresponding linear map. If $\bigoplus E_{\lambda_i}$ is not enough to cover $V$, we want to somehow expand each $E_{\lambda_i}$ into a bigger subspace, with the importance to keep their nice structure and disjointness. $\newline \newline$

The way to this is for each $E_{\lambda_i}$ to consider $$G_{\lambda_i}=\lim_{k\rightarrow  \infty } \ker(T-\lambda_i \mathrm{id})^k$$Denote $X_k=\ker(T-\lambda_i \mathrm{id})^k$ and notice that $E_{\lambda_i}=X_1$. It can be seen that $$X_k\subseteq X_{k+1}$$ which means that for each iteration of $k$, the subspace $X_k$ grows. Each $X_k$ has 2 nice properties: it is nilpotent under $T-\lambda_i\mathrm{id}$ (definition), and invariant under $T$ and $T-\lambda_i\mathrm{id}$ which actually follows from the former. $\newline$$\newline$
\textbf{Claim 1:} $X_k$ is invariant under $T-\lambda_i\mathrm{id}$ $\newline$
\textit{Proof}: Follows from $(\heartsuit)$. \hfill  $\blacksquare$ $\\$ $\\$
\textbf{Claim 2:} $X_k$ is invariant under $T$ $\newline$
\textit{Proof:} We use the invariance under $T-\lambda_i\mathrm{id}$: $$v\in X_k\implies T(v)-\lambda_i v\in X_k \implies T(v)\in X_k$$because of closeness. \hfill $\blacksquare$ $\\$ $\\$
Notice that because $X_k\subseteq X_{k+1}\subseteq V \ \forall k$, after some iteration $N$ we must have $$X_k=X_{k+1}\  \forall k\geq N$$since we are bounded by the dimension of $V$.
$\\$ $\\$ Our final extendable subset is $G_{\lambda_i}=X_N$. There is an obvious way to try to pick a basis for $G_{\lambda_i}$.
Let $$T_1(v) := T(v)-\lambda_i v$$ and recall each $v\in G_{\lambda_i}$ eventually gets mapped to $0$ under $T_1$ $\\$ $\\$
\textbf{Claim:} The vectors in the chain $v,T_1(v),..., T_1^k(v)$ are linearly independent where $T_1^{k+1}(v)=0$ and $k+1$ being the smallest integer with such property. $\\$
\textit{Proof:} Let $v=v_1$ and $v_{i+1}=T_1(v_i)$. We have$$c_1v_1+c_2v_2+...+c_kv_k=0.$$
Applying $T_1^{k}$ on both sides gives $$c_1v_k=0\implies c_1=0$$($v_k$ is not $0$ because of minimality of $k+1$).
Substituting back above and applying $T_1^{k-1}$ on both sides gives $$c_2v_k=0\implies c_2=0.$$
Thus, inductively we obtain $$c_1=c_2=...=c_k=0$$
\hfill $\blacksquare$ \\
So we can pick a chain of linearly independent vectors in $G_{\lambda_i}$ like above, however we do not know whether they span it. They usually don't. For example the matrix \[
A=\begin{pmatrix}
2&1&0\\
0&2&0\\
0&0&2
\end{pmatrix}
\]has $G_2$ with dimension $3$ for the eigenvalue $2$, however the longest null-chain is of length $2$ so one is not enough.\\ \\
The trick is to pick multiple such chains. In fact: \\ \\
